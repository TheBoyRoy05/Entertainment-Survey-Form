Fritz Haber

The man who saved billions and killed millions

Fritz Haber in the Lab

Summary

As the man who pulled bread and bombs out of the air with his revolutionary method of turning Nitrogen into Ammonia, Fritz Haber's research and inventions were monumental in not only agriculture, saving billions from starvation, but also in both World Wars, causing millions of deaths. As such, Haber's life is a tragedy, and yet that tragedy was necessary for our lives today. After all, an estimated 50% of the Nitrogen atoms in our bodies are a result of Haber's works. So here's tribute to the man who both gave and took countless lives.

Timeline of Fritz Haber's Life

  • 1868 - Born on December 9, 1868 in Breslau, Germany
  • 1886-1891 - Studied chemistry at the University of Heidelberg under Bunsen and then at the University of Berlin, under A.W. Hoffmann
  • 1894 - Haber accepted an assistantship at Karlsruhe by Professor Hans Bunte
  • 1896 - Honored for his thesis on experimental studies of the decomposition and combustion of hydrocarbons
  • 1898 - Published his textbook on Electrochemistry in 1898
  • 1901 - Married Clara Immerwahr, one of the first women to recieve a Ph.D. in chemistry
  • 1902 - Fritz's and Clara's son Hermann was born and they settled into Karlshruhe in southern Germany
  • 1905 - Published his book on The Thermodynamics of Techical Gas Reactions
  • 1906 - Became a professor of Physical Chemistry and Electrochemistry
  • 1909 - Invented a method to turn the nitrogen in the air into fertilizer, saving billions from starvation in the future
  • 1911 - Became director of the Institute for Physical and Electrochemistry at Berlin-Dahlem
  • 1915 - Helped develop chlorine gas as a chemical weapon against the Allies in WW1, causing his wife, a pacifist, to kill herself
  • 1917 - In his second marriage, Haber married Charlotte Nathan
  • 1918 - Haber was given The Noble Prize in Chemistry, but was shunned by his peers for his role in WW1
  • 1930 - Established the Japan Institute, located in both Berlin and Tokyo
  • 1933 - Haber's institute took a downfall with which he finally, and respectfully decided to resign his position
  • 1934 - Haber died on January 29, 1934, from a great illness in Basle
  • 1933-1945 - Haber's cyanide-based insectiside, Zyklon B, was repurposed to carry out the holocaust
